  • Late night snacking plays a role in obesity...

Late night snacking plays a role in obesity and weight gain, research finds.

· Regulation of metabolism by the circadian (sleep) cycle suggests that the body may change the way it digests food over the course of the day. Understanding how this works could yield profound insights into healthy eating habits and successful dieting.

· Skipping breakfast and replacing it with a late night snack resulted in less fat burned during sleep which contributes to weight gain, researchers at Vanderbilt University found.

· This discovery informs healthy eating habits and dieting and could help individuals struggling with weight loss make better decisions regarding their diets.

Eating a 3rd meal as a late night snack (in place of breakfast) resulted in decreased fat oxidation levels during subjects’ sleep—burning an average of 15g less fat over the course of a day, researchers at Vanderbilt University discovered.

 Western society faces an epidemic of obesity which poses a major risk to public health— increasing the prevalence of diabetes, heart disease and cancer. Diet and exercise are our main tools to combat this issue, but there is much interest in whether timing of meals has significant impact on weight gain. Previous research has investigated this subject over short time periods, using younger participants that do not accurately represent the portion of the population more likely to struggle with weight management.

However, Dr Kevin Parsons Kelly and his peers at Vanderbilt University published results in a paper titled “Eating breakfast and avoiding the evening snack sustains lipid oxidation” in the Nature journal Scientific Reports on the 20th of February, 2020 that determined the effect meal timing has on food metabolism over the course of several days.

Their study on middle-aged adults of varying BMI (Body Mass Index) found that when participants ate a late night snack in place of breakfast they burned an average 15g less fat each day.

Dr Owen P. McGuinness from the School of Medicine at Vanderbilt University comments:

“Understanding how our choices affect our health is very important. We need to know how to best inform the public about what the scientific community considers healthy eating habits and a key step in doing so is understanding how our metabolism is affected by our choices.”

“Previous studies investigating human metabolism have had monitoring periods of 24 hours or less—not enough time to investigate a phenomenon based on circadian rhythmicity (daily rhythm)”



The figure shows the schedules of the two, 56 hour sessions subjects took part in. Subjects were given lunch, dinner and a 3rd meal (served as breakfast or a late night snack) each day. The participants lived in a metabolic chamber (pictured below) for the duration of the study which measured how much oxygen they consumed, their temperature and other vitals which helped researched determine how their bodies were digesting food at any given moment.



The research shows how the late night snack influenced participants’ metabolisms to break down less fat over the course of the night. The figure below shows participants’ RER (Respiratory Exchange Ratio) scores over the course of the sessions. RER scores can inform researchers as to if the body is burning fat or carbohydrates at any given moment, with lower scores indicating the breakdown of fat.

Participants’ bodies broke down more fat during sleep (grey areas) when they had their third meal as breakfast (blue line) versus when they had their third meal as a late night snack (red line). This can be seen by the dip in RER scores during sleep in the breakfast session compared to the less pronounced dip during the late night snack sessions.

The late night meals resulted in an average of 15g less fat burned by participants bodies every day. While this may not sound like much, individuals who regularly skip breakfast and compensate with a late night snack are much more at risk of becoming obese and struggling with weight management. The researchers believe that the shortened period of time between the last meal of the day and bedrest pushes the body’s metabolism to burn carbs during sleep when it would otherwise be burning fat. They conclude that eating breakfast and avoiding the late night snack sustains lipid oxidation (breakdown of fat) during sleep.


Dr. Maciej Buchowski, Gastroenterologist of Vanderbilt University Medical Center comments:

“I would highly advise against skipping breakfast and eating late especially to individuals struggling to lose weight. These data only proves what we already suspected however this phenomenon is most definitely not the leading factor contributing to obesity in this country—A well balanced diet and regular exercise are far more important in effective weight management.”

NOTES TO EDITORS

Vanderbilt University, a private research institution in Nashville, Tennessee is classified by the Carnegie Foundation as a Doctoral University with Highest Research Activity. Research covers an assortment of sectors but they frequently collaborate (as in this study) with the now-independent Vanderbilt University Medical Center on research of high relevance in the medical field.
